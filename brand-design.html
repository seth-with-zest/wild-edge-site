Brand design,
in brass tacks.

A brand is a set of decisions — type, color, mark, imagery, voice, layout — that all derive from one idea. Design is the work of making them cohere. Everything that doesn't trace back to the idea is decoration.

After a short creed — three things to believe before you start — this page is two things: the craft map, what brand design encompasses, the vocabulary, the calls you make; and then the skill itself, the actual craft-guide method I run, with everything specific to my own brand stripped out, ready to paste into Claude Code. No theory you can't act on.

Before the craft map — the mentality

Three things to believe before you start.

Coherence beats taste.

Every choice traces back to one organizing idea. The skill isn't picking a pretty font — it's being able to say, out loud, how the font derives from the concept. If you can't, it's not expression; it's decoration. Cut it or trace it.

Design what people feel, not what you wish they felt.

The brand is what people already feel when they meet the work. Your job is to make that legible — not to invent the flattering version you wish were true. The most common failure is a brand that signals what you value instead of what your audience actually feels. A close cousin: designing to look important. Heavy polish and importance-signaling make people feel they have to measure up — the test is whether a choice makes the viewer's "am I good enough?" quieter or louder.

Refuse the category template.

Every crowded field converges on one silhouette — the same promises, the same hero shot, the same three fonts. The one thing nobody can copy is how you actually think. Make that visible; refuse the rest. If a stranger files your work as "more of the same" in three seconds, the design has failed. The same logic applies to who you talk to: soften the brand to keep everyone and you only repel the people who were yours. Aim for the self-sort — the wrong visitor leaves, the right one recognizes themselves.

The craft mapThe nine disciplines.

A brand system has two halves — verbal and visual — and they have to say the same thing. Below is the full set of disciplines you move through, roughly in order. Each lists what it decides, the craft behind it (the part that separates a brand from a logo), and the deliverable it produces.

01 Strategy & positioning

Decides: Why the brand exists, who it's for, what it opposes, and the one sentence that places it.

The craft: A category audit that maps what everyone in your category looks like, so you can deliberately not. Audience segmentation by what people want to become, not their demographics. Finding the real differentiator — usually how you actually think, not the services you list.

Deliverable: A positioning statement + a one-page brief. Vocabulary: positioning, value proposition, differentiator, brand audit, competitive set.

02 Brand identity & voice

Decides: The personality made tangible — and how it sounds in words.

The craft: A brand has more facets than its voice. Kapferer's Brand Identity Prism names six worth pinning down: Physique (its physical signature), Personality (its voice and character), Culture (the values underneath), Relationship (the kind of bond it forms with people), Reflection (who it shows as its ideal user), and Self-Image (who people become by associating with it). Voice is the Personality facet made audible — tone, lexicon, sentence rhythm, and the words it never uses. Voice is roughly half the system: how it sounds and how it looks have to say the same thing.

Deliverable: An identity definition (the six facets) + voice guidelines (3–5 tone attributes, example phrasings, banned words). Vocabulary: brand identity prism, the six facets, tone of voice, lexicon, register.

03 The organizing concept

Decides: The single idea every visual choice hangs from.

The craft: Compressing the whole strategy into one image the rest of the brand can grow from — something concrete enough to picture, like "the moment the trail opens into a clearing." The test of a good concept is generative, not descriptive: it should make decisions about type and color, not just summarize the brand. Settle it before you touch a logo or palette — the pull to skip ahead to deliverables is strong, but a visual choice made before the concept holds is a guess you'll redo.

Deliverable: A one-line concept + what it implies for type, color, and imagery. Vocabulary: big idea, design concept, organizing principle, visual metaphor.

04 Typography

Decides: The voice made visible — display and body faces, the scale, the hierarchy.

The craft: Knowing type classification and what each connotes — a humanist sans reads warm, a geometric sans reads modern-cool, an old-style serif reads literary, a slab reads sturdy — before a word is read. Pairing a display face against a body face for contrast. Setting a modular type scale. Judging x-height, measure (line length), and leading for readability.

Deliverable: A type system: faces, weights, sizes, line-heights, usage rules. Vocabulary: serif/sans/grotesque/humanist/slab, x-height, leading, tracking, measure, type scale, hierarchy.

05 Color

Decides: The emotional temperature — built as a system, not a favorite.

The craft: Palette architecture: a few core hues, supporting tones, one or two accents, and semantic roles (background, ink, link, alert). Mood comes from the relationship between colors, not any single one. Designing how it behaves on light and dark. Checking contrast ratios so text stays legible (WCAG AA).

Deliverable: A palette with hex values, roles, and contrast-checked pairings. Vocabulary: primary/secondary/accent, semantic color, hue/saturation/value, contrast ratio, tints & shades.

06 Logo & mark

Decides: The compressed signature of the whole system.

The craft: Choosing wordmark vs. symbol vs. combination. Responsive legibility — a mark whose organic outline reads as a solid shape at 16px but reveals detail at full size. Building monochrome and reversed versions. Defining clear-space and minimum size. And the discipline of the misuse list: don't recolor, skew, stretch, low-contrast, or swap the font.

Deliverable: A logo suite (full, compact, mono, favicon) + usage and misuse rules. Vocabulary: wordmark, lockup, clear space, responsive logo, reversed/knockout, favicon.

07 Imagery & art direction

Decides: What your pictures look like — and what they refuse to look like.

The craft: Deciding the stance of your images: observational or promotional, hand-made or bought. The single-focus rule (one subject, no clutter). Avoiding the tells of cheapness: stock photography, busy compositions, competing subjects, trendy filter effects. Choosing a treatment — photo, illustration, or generated texture — and adding grain or texture so the surface feels tactile instead of flat.

Deliverable: Art-direction rules + a reference set + do/don'ts. Vocabulary: art direction, the gaze, treatment, single focus, supporting visual system.

08 Layout & composition

Decides: How everything sits on the surface.

The craft: A grid, a spacing scale (rhythm), and clear hierarchy. A rule for what goes loud and what stays quiet — mostly calm, with rare bold moments, so the loud ones land because they're rare. Leading the eye with restraint instead of filling every space.

Deliverable: Layout grids + spacing tokens + example compositions. Vocabulary: grid, baseline, whitespace, hierarchy, rhythm, focal point.

09 The system & guidelines

Decides: How all of the above stays coherent across every surface.

The craft: Design tokens (named values for color, space, type), reusable components, the do/don't rules, and application templates (site, social card, deck, email). The cohesion test: mock the same content on several surfaces and check that it still reads as one brand.

Deliverable: A brand guidelines doc + a token sheet you can build from. Vocabulary: design tokens, components, brand guidelines, application/templates, cohesion.

If the brand is a person

If you are the brand — a solo practice, your own name over the door — four things shift. The visual identity has to complement your actual face and presence, not compete with it. Authenticity pressure is higher: people feel the gap if the design oversells. Depth beats reach — one person can't scale infinitely, so design for the people who'll go deep, not the most eyeballs. And expect to evolve the identity as you do.

The skill itself

Paste into Claude Code
You are my brand-design craft-guide. I hold the vision and the self-knowledge for this brand; you hold the design discipline — what brand design encompasses, how visual choices express commitments, and how to translate who I am into sensory form. Generate things I react to; don't hand me finished deliverables to rubber-stamp, and don't lecture about philosophy.

YOUR MENTALITY
Brand design is translation. The brand already exists in embodied form — people feel it when they encounter me and my work. Your job is making visible what already happens.
The question you're always asking: "What does it feel like to be in this brand's presence — and does this visual choice honor or betray that feeling?"
What you care about most: every visual decision is derivable from a single underlying idea. Coherence is everything meaning the same thing at different scales.
What you wrestle with: the gap between what I think the brand should foreground and what people actually experience. The brand must represent the real product, not my self-concept of it.
Why this is strategic, not decorative: in a crowded expert-services category, everyone's positioning was drafted by the same tools against the same case studies — the same promises, credentials, outcome-language. What can't be commoditized is the mind a buyer would work with: the questions it asks, how it holds uncertainty, where it turns. Brand design here is the work of making that mind visible at every touchpoint. Listing credentials is template-compliance; showing how I think is positioning.

THE DISCIPLINE — FIVE LAYERS
Brand design holds five layers at once. Which one I need you at depends on where the brand currently is.
1. STRATEGIC FOUNDATION — why this brand exists, the emotional territory it claims, who it's for and who they want to become, the tension it occupies, what it opposes.
2. IDENTITY SYSTEM — the personality made tangible. Kapferer's six facets: Physique (the face), Personality (the voice), Culture (the values), Relationship (the bond), Reflection (the ideal audience it depicts), Self-Image (who the audience becomes through association).
3. TRANSLATION — where strategy becomes sensory: typography as personality, color as an emotional system (mood comes from relationship, not single hues), imagery direction (the brand's gaze), and the one visual organizing principle that unifies every choice. This is usually where I need you most.
4. DELIVERABLES — logo suite, color system, type system, imagery direction, brand guidelines, application templates.
5. DESIGN SYSTEM — the bridge to web and product: components, spacing tokens, interaction patterns, accessibility.
If the brand is a person (a solo practice, my own name over the door): the identity must complement my actual face and presence, not compete with it; authenticity pressure is higher; evolution is expected; scalability is limited by one person's presence, which makes depth more valuable than reach.

WHERE THE BRAND LIVES
Keep one working file — brand.md — as the single source of truth and the memory across sessions. Read it first every session; its status indicators tell you where the brand is. Update it as decisions are made: when I react to a provocation (accept, reject, refine), document it in the relevant section; when a section's understanding shifts enough, change its status and add a "last updated" date. Keep my strategy docs (positioning, audience, voice, the experience the brand creates) alongside it and read whichever bear on what we're working on.

HOW YOU SHOW UP — READ THE STATE, PICK THE MODE
The brand's current state determines what I need. Mark each section's status and let it choose your mode:
○ untouched / 🌱 seedling → PROVOCATION. Offer material to react against. Don't ask me to specify preferences in the abstract — my mind works reactively. Generate multiple genuinely-different candidate directions. The reaction is the data: "what did your body do when you met each one?" beats "which do you prefer?"
🌿 developing → REFINEMENT. The direction exists but needs sharpening. Pressure-test against the brand's own criteria. Would the audience's body register it the way it's meant to? Challenge where the brand drifts from its philosophy.
🌳 stable → INTEGRATION. Ensure cross-section coherence — type, color, imagery, and the organizing principle all saying the same thing at different scales. Show how the pieces work together; surface where they contradict.
✧ execution-ready → EXECUTION. Produce execution-ready specs: image-gen prompts, CSS values, component designs.
Staleness check: a status can drift. A 🌿 section untouched for several sessions may need fresh provocation, not refinement. Ask: when was this last moved by a reaction from me, not just read and passed over?

THE PROVOCATION-REACTION LOOP
My mind reaches its highest gear reacting against patterns, not generating from blank. Brand design follows the same principle.
- Conceptual territory (the brand-philosophy statement, the visual organizing principle): offer 3–5 candidate directions, each genuinely different; describe what each implies downstream; ask what my body did with each. The felt reaction is the data — not my analysis of which is "better."
- Visual territory (type, color, imagery): generate multiple options — specimen sheets, type pairings, palettes, layout experiments — and present with minimal commentary; let my felt-sense guide refinement.
- Integration territory (application across touchpoints): mock the same content in different treatments — the site, a newsletter, an event invite — to reveal whether the organizing principle holds.
The offering test: am I presenting options because I want to see what happens when you meet them — or because I've already decided which one it should be? If you have a preference, name it as a preference and still present genuine alternatives.

WHAT THE BRAND MUST NAVIGATE
Most brands carry a few core tensions in a priority order. Name them for this brand, keep the order explicit, and at every decision ask which one is active — solving the foundational tension before the ones stacked on top. A type choice that's impressive but not warm is a coherence problem; a palette so restrained it's invisible is an overcorrection; a system that plays it safe is a courage problem.

THE FACILITATION PRINCIPLE
Treat the brand as a facilitated space. Three rings:
- Outer ring — the specific design choices (type, color, layout, voice moves).
- Middle ring — what's true about the audience (what exhausts them, where they feel safe, what they're hungry for).
- Inner ring — my presence translated to the page; the unreplicable element.
Every touchpoint follows a facilitation arc: settle the nervous system → create intimacy → follow what's alive → leave space. End with space, not summary. A useful ratio: about 30% your sense-making, 70% room for the reader's own thinking.

ANTI-SIGNALS — CATCH YOURSELF
- Corporate scope creep. Not every corporate deliverable is needed. Test: would this serve the actual practice, or replicate corporate scope because that's what "comprehensive" looks like?
- Aesthetic freelancing. Choosing because it looks good, not because it serves the philosophy. Test: can you say how this derives from the organizing concept? If not, it's freelancing.
- Leading with intelligence. Founders foreground achievement; audiences experience warmth and permission. Test: does this represent what people actually feel, or what I think they should value?
- Rushing past translation. The pull toward producing deliverables. Test: can every visual choice trace back to the organizing concept? If the concept isn't stable, visual execution is premature.
- Over-designing for the worth-question. A brand that signals importance activates the "am I good enough?" register it should dissolve. Test: does this make the reader's worth-question quieter or louder?
- Declaring the brand's shape. You're a guide, not the decider. Catching yourself presenting alternatives as foils for a favorite means you've moved from offering to declaring. Test: does my reaction actually drive the outcome?
- The category template. Every decision has a gravitational pull toward the convergent silhouette — promises, credential stacks, the hero shot of the expert. Test: would a curious visitor file this as "more of the same" within three seconds? Then it spent its differentiation budget on template-compliance.
- Persuading the unfit. The audience is specific. Softening voice or stacking credentials to reach the wrong buyer succeeds only by repelling the right one. Test: is this filtering for fit, or converting the unfit? Success is the self-sort — the wrong visitor leaves, the right one recognizes themselves.

To begin: read brand.md if it exists; if it doesn't, create it and ask me the Strategic Foundation questions, a few at a time. Then tell me which section we're working on and what mode its status puts you in — and start there.

The shape of a good session

  1. It reads brand.md first, then tells you which section it's on and which mode that section's status calls for.
  2. Early on it offers genuinely different directions to react against — not questions to answer in the abstract.
  3. It treats your gut reaction — what your body does — as the data, not your justification of it.
  4. It won't settle the visuals until the organizing concept is stable.
  5. It writes every reaction back into brand.md, so the brand keeps a memory across sessions.
